Opioid Epidemic Network Modeling

Modeling High-Rate Opioid Prescribing as Spatial Contagion Across U.S. States and Counties
Vishal Maradana & Rishi Bandi | Georgia Institute of Technology
Introduction

The opioid crisis has killed over 500,000 Americans since the late 1990s. Traditional approaches treat regions independently, overlooking how prescribing behaviors may spread across boundaries.

Research Question: Does high-rate opioid prescribing diffuse geographically across U.S. states and counties like a contagion?

Key Insight: Healthcare practices travel via professional networks, hospital cultures, and regional policies—suggesting prescribing norms may spread spatially.

Objectives:

  • Infer directed influence networks identifying early adopter regions
  • Predict future prescribing rates using spatial autoregressive models
  • Compare dynamics at state vs. county scales
Methodology

1. High-Prescribing Classification

  • State-level: Rate > 87.35 per 100 persons (75th percentile)
  • County-level: State-specific 75th percentile thresholds

2. Influence Network Construction

For consecutive years (t, t+1), edges drawn from existing high-prescribers to new adopters among geographic neighbors:

w(s→n) = Σ 1/(1 + (t - ts))

3. Spatial Autoregressive (SAR) Model

Rs,t+1 = α + β·Rs,t + γ·R̄N(s),t + ε

β

Self-Persistence

γ

Spatial Spillover

4. Influence Ranking

Weighted out-degree centrality: Cout(s) = Σ w(s→n)

Key Findings
  • High Path Dependence: Self-persistence dominates (β > 0.95) — regions that become high-prescribers tend to remain so.
  • Measurable Spatial Spillover: Neighbor effects are small but detectable, stronger at county level (5%) than state level (1%).
  • Top State Influencers: Tennessee, Nevada, and South Carolina consistently preceded neighboring states in adoption.
  • Early Hotspots: 345 superspreader counties identified; Montana counties led by 6 years.
Conclusion

Conclusions:

  • High-prescribing behavior exhibits spatial contagion patterns across U.S. states and counties.
  • Prevention of initial high-rate adoption is more effective than reversing established patterns.
  • Results support regionally coordinated interventions over isolated state-by-state approaches.
