Opioid Epidemic Network Modeling

Analyzing the Spatial and Temporal Spread of High-Prescribing Behaviors

Project Summary

This project uses network science and spatial modeling to analyze the US Opioid Epidemic as a diffusion process. By analyzing CDC dispensing rate data from 2006 to 2018, we constructed influence networks at both state and county levels to uncover how high-prescribing behaviors spread across geographic borders.

Our approach combines two techniques: (1) influence network construction to identify which regions preceded others in adopting high-prescribing behavior, and (2) spatial autoregressive models to predict future prescribing rates and quantify spatial spillover effects.

Key Results

  • Strong Prediction Performance: Our spatial autoregressive model achieves R² = 0.884 at state level and R² = 0.792 at county level, demonstrating that future prescribing rates can be reliably predicted from historical patterns and geographic context.
  • High Path Dependence: Prescribing behavior is highly persistent (β = 0.981 at state level, β = 0.950 at county level). Regions that become high-prescribers tend to remain so, with modest but measurable neighbor spillover effects.
  • Key Influencers Identified: Tennessee emerged as the primary state-level influencer (highest Out-Degree weight of 1.67), followed by Nevada and South Carolina. The Southeast forms a densely connected high-prescribing cluster.
  • Superspreader Counties: We identified 345 counties that adopted high-prescribing rates before their states crossed the threshold. Montana counties led by 6 years, followed by Kansas (5 years) and Idaho (4 years)—these early hotspots represent potential targets for early intervention.
